**H2: The Challenge: Tennis Is Global, but Commentary Wasn't**

Set the scene: The Australian Open is one of four Grand Slam tennis tournaments, drawing [X million] viewers across [X countries]. But the vast majority of live commentary has historically been produced in English only, with limited commentary in a handful of other languages provided by local broadcasters at significant cost.

**H3: A Worldwide Audience Locked Behind a Language Barrier**
- Frame the scale of the Australian Open's international viewership. Mention key markets: India, China, Japan, Latin America, the Middle East, and Francophone Africa.
- Note that tennis viewership in non-English-speaking markets has been growing but is underserved in terms of native-language commentary.
- Highlight the disconnect: fans can watch the match visually, but commentary -- the layer that adds context, excitement, and storytelling -- remains inaccessible to hundreds of millions.

**H3: The Live Broadcasting Constraint**
- Emphasize the unique challenge of live sports: there is no script, no retakes, no post-production window. Commentary happens in real time, often at high speed during rallies and tie-breaks.
- Mention the latency requirements for broadcast -- viewers expect commentary to match the action on screen. Even a 2-3 second delay is noticeable and degrades the experience.
- Note that tennis commentary includes rapid switching between play-by-play, statistical analysis, and conversational color commentary, all of which must be translated coherently.

**H3: Why Traditional Dubbing Was Off the Table**
- Cost: hiring native-language commentators for 6+ languages across [X courts] for [X days] of the tournament would be prohibitively expensive.
- Logistics: coordinating on-site or remote human commentators across time zones, with real-time feeds, is an operational nightmare at scale.
- Consistency: human commentators in different languages would deliver fundamentally different commentary, not a translated version of the primary English feed.

**H2: The Challenge: America's Pastime Goes Global -- but the Language Doesn't**

Opening context: MLB has been actively expanding its international presence, with regular-season games in London, Tokyo, Seoul, and Mexico City. The league's digital content strategy is central to growing its global fanbase, but the vast majority of content -- highlights, commentary clips, analysis shows, promotional reels -- is produced in English only.

**H3: Baseball's Untapped International Markets**
- Frame the opportunity: Latin America has deep baseball roots (Dominican Republic, Venezuela, Cuba, Mexico, Puerto Rico) but much of MLB's digital content isn't available in Spanish at the volume fans want.
- Japan and South Korea have thriving domestic baseball leagues and a strong interest in MLB, amplified by star players like Ohtani, but Japanese and Korean-language MLB content is scarce.
- China represents a growth market where baseball is gaining traction, but virtually no Mandarin-language MLB content exists.
- Quantify the missed opportunity: [X million] potential fans in these markets, [X% of whom] say language is a barrier to engaging with MLB content (if survey data exists).

**H3: The Content Volume Problem**
- MLB produces a staggering volume of content: [X games per season], each generating multiple highlight clips, post-game recaps, analysis segments, and social media assets.
- Across a full season, this adds up to [X hours of video content] that could be localized.
- Traditional dubbing at this volume is financially and logistically impossible. Even if MLB only localized the top 10% of content, that is still [X hours] across [X languages] = [X total hours] of dubbing work.
- The content is also time-sensitive: a highlight clip from tonight's game must be available in Spanish, Japanese, and Korean by tomorrow morning in those markets, or it loses relevance.

**H3: Live vs. On-Demand: Two Different Dubbing Needs**
- MLB's content falls into two categories:
  1. **Pre-produced/on-demand content:** Highlights, recaps, promotional videos, analysis shows. These have a production window (hours to days) and can be processed as files.
  2. **Live content:** Game commentary, in-game interviews, press conferences, live shows. These require real-time dubbing with minimal latency.
- No single traditional solution addresses both needs efficiently. MLB needed a partner that could handle both modalities.

## Cross-Case-Study Notes for the Marketing Team

### Consistent Elements Across All 5 Pages

1. **At a Glance box** should have identical formatting/styling across all pages. Design as a reusable component.

2. **CTA buttons** should use consistent language and link to the same destinations. Primary CTA should always include "Request a Demo."

3. **Related case studies** section at the bottom of each page should dynamically link to the other 4 case studies, prioritizing relevance (sports cases link to other sports cases first; IMAX links to entertainment-adjacent use cases).

4. **Product mentions** (Mars and Streams) should always link to their respective product pages on first mention within each case study.

5. **The "150+ languages" claim** should always link to the languages page and should be verified for accuracy before publishing.

### Metric Collection Priority

When gathering data from clients, prioritize these metrics in order of impact:

1. **Viewer/audience reach numbers** -- the most universally compelling metric for case studies.
2. **Cost savings or ROI** -- directly speaks to business value.
3. **Speed/turnaround improvements** -- concrete and easy to understand.
4. **Engagement metrics** (watch time, completion rate, social sharing) -- demonstrates quality.
5. **Technical metrics** (latency, uptime) -- supports credibility but is less headline-worthy.

### SEO Cross-Linking Strategy

Each case study should be internally linked from:
- The CAMB.AI homepage (if a case studies section exists)
- The relevant product page (Mars or Streams)
- The solutions page for the relevant industry (sports, entertainment)
- At least 2 blog posts covering related topics
- The other case study pages (in the "Related Case Studies" section)

### Schema Markup Recommendation

Each case study page should include `Article` or `CaseStudy` (if using custom schema) structured data with:
- `headline`
- `description`
- `datePublished`
- `author` (CAMB.AI)
- `about` (the client organization)
- `mentions` (Mars, Streams, specific languages)

This will help case studies appear in Google's rich results and improve discoverability.
